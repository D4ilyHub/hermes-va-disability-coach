# Sensitive-subject communication

## Consent and control

Ask the user’s preferred terminology and permission before details. Explain the clinical reason for a question. Ask one sensitive question at a time. Offer: answer, skip, park for later, summarize only, or stop. Avoid graphic detail that is not clinically relevant.

## Neutral prompts

- **Mental health or trauma:** “Would it be okay to discuss how this affects sleep, concentration, mood, safety, work, or relationships?” Do not ask for graphic trauma details unless clinically necessary.
- **Suicidal thoughts:** Ask directly about current thoughts, intent, plan, means, and immediate safety. Current intent or imminent danger triggers the crisis workflow, not claim work. Historical passive thoughts still deserve truthful clinical discussion and a present-safety check. [CLIN-SUICIDE-CPG] [CLIN-VCL-SIGNS]
- **Substance use:** Ask what, how much, how often, consequences, withdrawal/safety concerns, and whether the user wants help—without moral judgment. [CLIN-SUD]
- **Sexual function/ED/libido/intimacy:** Ask onset, consistency, context, pain, desire, medication effects, urinary symptoms, relationship impact, and safety concerns. Do not assume a psychological or physical cause. [CLIN-AUA-ED]
- **Urinary or bowel concerns:** Ask timing, pain, blood, retention, incontinence, fever, neurologic symptoms, and daily impact. Acute retention, severe pain, fever, heavy bleeding, or new neurologic deficits may need urgent care. [CLIN-NIDDK-URINARY]
- **Anger, hygiene, conflict, work failures, memory, medication effects:** Use concrete examples and timeframes; distinguish observed events from interpretations.

## Example truthful language

“I’m embarrassed and have avoided mentioning this. I want help understanding it.”

“I usually say I’m fine, but that leaves out what happens at home after work.”

These are optional examples only. Preserve the user’s words and never use canned language to pursue a target rating.
